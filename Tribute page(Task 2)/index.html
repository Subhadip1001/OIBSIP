Swami Vivekananda

Swami Vivekananda, born Narendranath Datta on January 12, 1863, in Kolkata, was a prominent Hindu monk and spiritual leader. He was a key disciple of the mystic Ramakrishna and played a crucial role in introducing Indian philosophies of Vedanta and Yoga to the Western world.
Vivekananda is perhaps best known for his inspiring speech at the Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago in 1893, where he began with the words "Sisters and brothers of America..." This speech not only brought him international fame but also highlighted the universal message of Hinduism.
He founded the Ramakrishna Mission in 1897, which focuses on social service and spiritual development. His teachings emphasized the importance of selfless service, the unity of all religions, and the potential divinity of every individual.
Swami Vivekananda's legacy continues to inspire millions around the world. His message of universal brotherhood and selfless service remains relevant today, encouraging people to strive for personal and societal betterment.
